Get Diagnosed

This is not medical advice. It is not written by a medical professional. You must fully research any pertinent information and speak to a licensed medical professional before taking any medical decisions, undergoing a procedure, or taking a new medication.

This resource is written for those aged 18 and above, who have the legal capacity to make fully independent decisions about their health.

Advice for those below the age of 18 can be found on the "Advice for Parents and Guardians" section of this site, though we do still recommend reading this guide as there are relevant details.

Advice for Parents and Guardians

Purpose

To safely and legally undertake most gender-related surgeries, as well as get a prescription for HRT, you will need a formal diagnosis from a medical professional.

This diagnosis will tend to be for gender dysphoria, but it may be for one of several similar conditions.

Please note, being trans or gender nonconforming is not a mental illness or disorder, and you do not need a diagnosis of any sort for your identity to be valid. A diagnosis is often required for access to medical care and support.

Diagnostic Routes

NHS

Cheaper but slower.

Wait times for appointments at Gender Identity Clinics may exceed five years, despite the target being only 18 weeks. Src: BBC, NHS

You can either get a referral from your GP, or you can self-refer directly to a Gender Identity Clinic (GIC). GP referrals are often faster as they can also supply your full medical history. Src: NHS

Find a GIC

The GIC covering the London area is run by the Tavistock and Portman NHS Foundation Trust. They provide a self-referral form to download and fill in.

This clinic reportedly has over a 6-year wait time for new patients. Src: TransActual

Once you have a diagnosis from an NHS GIC, most of your gender-affirming care such as surgery or HRT will be funded by the NHS.

Private

Expensive but quicker.

In the interest of responsibility and liability, SafeT cannot recommend specific private healthcare providers, nor do we endorse any particular firm.

The advice below is general and you are strongly encouraged to do your own research on services that suit you.

Like reading this 2024 review of various providers.

Private gender identity services may not be a 1:1 substitute for NHS-provided services, but may be adequate in supporting an individual while on a waiting list for NHS services.

Many services offer assessments and diagnoses, which can by used to access further medical services as well as carry out other important changes such as getting a GRC or updating your passport.

Shared Care

An April 2025 guidance document issued by NHS England tells NHS GPs that they must not support prescription and supply of HRT from private providers. This document further states that GPs "may decline to accept responsibility for prescribing, monitoring and testing if the GP is not assured that the provider offers a safe service". Src: NHS

This means that, if you are prescribed HRT by a private provider, your GP may refuse to carry out liver function tests or other monitoring, which is necessary for safe HRT use.

However, if your GP is willing to support any private care, then your provider and your GP will need to organise a shared care agreement, which is a formal agreement between the two parties to share responsibility for your care. This may also mean that your prescription will be charged to you at NHS prices (currently £9.90 maximum per item) rather than the private cost. Src: NHS, NHS

Fortunately, Chelsea and Westminster Hospital NHS Foundation Trust operates a sexual health clinic—56 Dean Street—which also offers services specific to trans and non-binary people. This includes liver function tests and hormone injections (once prescribed by a UK registered medical doctor). Appointments must be booked online or over the phone. Src: NHS

Cost

NHS

In short, free.
In long, a portion of your taxes and NI contributions if you're a British taxpayer, but you'd be paying those anyway.

Private

The cost of an assessment to get a diagnosis can vary between providers, but tends to be around £200–£500 per appointment. Some providers only need one appointment, some may need up to six.

Further care costs can also vary wildly between providers. They're usually broken up into two parts:

  • Initial Consultation/Upfront Fee
    • c. £200–£500
    • The one-time cost of your initial appointment.
  • Recurring Subscription/Prescription Fee
    • c. £40–£100
    • The cost for each of your monthly/quarterly review appointments and/or the cost of issuing a prescription.
